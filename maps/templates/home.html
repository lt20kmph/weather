1. About

The aim of this site is to provide a way to visually explore various datasets freely available from the Met Office. Five categories of data are included:

    {% load tags %}
  • Sunshine
  • Rainfall
  • Air temperature (Max)
  • Air temperature (Min)
  • Windspeed

The data provided by the Met Office at various resolution grids covering the UK. For this site I choose to work with the 25km resolution data as a reasonable compromise between accuracy and having a reasonable quantity of data to deal with. The air temperature and rainfall data spans the period 1884-2018, the sunshine data is for the period 1929-2018 and the windspeed data exists for 1969-2018.

The main function of the site is to provide a clickable map so that the weather data for a particular location can be examined. Along with a plot of the raw data, the 21 year centred moving average is also plotted (see below for more details).

The purpose of this webpage is not to draw conclusions or do a deep data analysis but simply allow the perusal of freely available data in a way that is hopefully digestible to most humans.

2. Annual vs. monthly data

The data is provided in annual or monthly records. The annual data allows comparing amongst years. The monthly data allows comparisons between specific months of the year. For example, using the monthly data you can compare all of the Septembers for which data exist to see if they are getting sunnier/wetter/colder/etc... Note that other comparisons would be possible using the monthly data, but as far as I can tell it is most interesting to compare Septembers with Septembers and so that is what I have chosen to do here.

3. The 21 year centred moving average (CMA21)

The 21 year centred moving average for a year is calculated by averaging the 21 year period centred at that year. In other words $$\mathrm{CMA21}(y)=\frac{\sum_{i=-10}^{10}\mathrm{data}(y+i)}{21},$$ where \(\mathrm{CMA21}(y)\) is the value of the 21 year centred moving average at the year \(y\) and \(\mathrm{data}(y)\) is the value of the data at the year \(y\). Moving averages are frequently applied to noisy time series (for example in financial markets) to remove local fluctuations and as a way to visualise underlying trends. See wikipedia for more details.

4. Correlations

Two different ways to compare across different data sets are provided. You can navigate between normal mode and the two different types of comparison using the menu in the upper right.

4.1. Comparing the same statistic at two locations

Select two locations using the map and see if the weather in the selected locations are correlated. Obviously locations which are close together are quite strongly correlated and those which are far apart, less so.

4.2. Comparing two statistics at the same place

Select two statistics using the menus and a location on the map and you can see if there are any correlations. For example you might like to investigate the possibility that less sun usually goes along with lower temperatures.

4.3. The correlation coefficient and linear regression line

The correlation coefficient, or more precisely Pearson's r-value is a measure of the linear correlation between two data sets. It takes values between -1 and 1 and a value of -1 means the two data sets are exactly negatively correlated and a value of 1 means the two data sets are in perfect positive linear correlation. See wikipedia for more information. The correlation coefficient is the slope of the linear regression line (line of best fit) which is the line that minimises the total sum of distances from the line to all of the points in the scatter plot.
